Teacher Interview Synthesis

What do teachers do when their assessment data tells them some students didn't get it?
✏️  Prepared by Mary Gurley
📅  Date April 2026
🎤  Method 30-min structured interviews · 5 teachers
🏫  Participants K–5 classroom teachers · All active CharacterStrong users
🏷️  Status In Progress
💡
Research question: When assessment data shows that some students didn't grasp a skill, how do teachers decide what to do next — reteach the same lesson, try a different approach, or escalate to Tier 2 support?

🔁 Recurring Theme
Heard across all 5 interviews
Teachers see the data. They don't know what to do with it — so they move on, and some students never get the instruction they needed.
Across every interview, the same gap surfaced: teachers can collect assessment data, and many of them do look at it — but the data doesn't translate into an instructional decision. When exit tickets show that some students didn't grasp a skill, teachers are left asking themselves: Do I reteach the same lesson? Try a different approach? Pull a small group? Move a student to Tier 2? Nothing in the platform answers that question.

This is different from not having data. The problem isn't missing information — it's a missing bridge between the data and the right instructional response. In academic subjects, teachers have a toolkit: small groups, differentiated texts, intervention programs. For SEL, that toolkit doesn't exist in the same accessible form. So even teachers who are actively looking at assessment results end up defaulting to the next lesson in the sequence — not because they don't care, but because the platform gives them no guidance on how to change instruction for students who aren't there yet.
The gap shows up across the full user spectrum
Hesitant (Ms. Halloway)
Has seen data. Doesn't know if it means reteach, differentiate, or refer. Does nothing.
Casual (Ms. Meyer, Mr. Washington, Ms. Patel)
Collects data and looks at it. Doesn't know what instructional move it calls for. Moves on anyway.
Power user (Mr. Ramirez)
Makes judgment calls on instructional response. Acknowledges they're guesses. No platform guidance.
The finding: The gap is not data collection — it's data interpretation. Teachers across every experience level and usage pattern face the same moment of paralysis: I can see that some kids didn't get it. I don't know what I'm supposed to do about it. Without a clear bridge from assessment result to instructional action, the default is always to move forward.
➡️ Implication for Product
What this means for the assessment platform
The opportunity is not to build a better data dashboard. Teachers aren't failing to find the data — they're failing to know what it means instructionally. The opportunity is to close the gap between assessment result and instructional action: to tell a teacher not just that four students struggled, but what to do about it. Reteach with a different approach? Try the Tier 2 support activity? Move on with a targeted check-in for those students? That translation — from data to decision — is what's missing. The platform has the data. It needs to speak the language of instruction.
